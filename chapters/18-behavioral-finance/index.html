Chapter 18: Behavioral Finance & Avoiding Scams

Summary

This chapter reveals the cognitive biases that silently cost you money and teaches you how to recognize and avoid financial scams. You'll learn about anchoring bias, loss aversion, confirmation bias, emotional spending triggers, and lifestyle inflation. You'll also learn to identify phishing attempts, protect yourself from identity theft, and spot social media financial misinformation.

Introduction: Your Brain Is Working Against Your Wallet

You have made it to Chapter 18. By now you understand budgets, investing, debt, and retirement accounts. You have real tools. But here is the uncomfortable truth: knowing the right financial move and actually making it are two very different things.

Your brain evolved to keep you alive on the savanna, not to evaluate a 401(k) plan. The same mental shortcuts that helped your ancestors avoid predators now cause you to panic-sell stocks, overpay for things you do not need, and fall for scams that seem obviously fake -- until you are the one receiving them.

Behavioral finance is the study of how psychology and emotions affect financial decisions. It combines traditional finance with cognitive psychology to explain why smart people do dumb things with money. This is not about intelligence. Doctors, engineers, and professors fall for the same traps. The difference is whether you learn to recognize the traps before they spring.

In this chapter, you will learn:

  • The specific cognitive biases that drain your bank account
  • How emotional spending works and what triggers it
  • Why raises often leave you no richer (lifestyle inflation)
  • How to spot financial scams, phishing, and identity theft
  • Why social media is one of the most dangerous places for financial advice

Cognitive Biases: The Glitches in Your Financial Brain

A cognitive bias is a systematic error in thinking that affects your decisions and judgments. Think of biases as bugs in your brain's software. Everyone has them. They are not signs of stupidity -- they are features of being human. But when they collide with money, they get expensive fast.

Let us walk through the three biases that cost young adults the most money.

Anchoring Bias

Anchoring bias is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information you see when making a decision. Your brain "anchors" to that number, and everything after it gets judged relative to that anchor -- even if the anchor is meaningless.

How it costs you money: A jacket has a tag reading "~~$200~~ NOW $89." Your brain sees a $111 discount. You feel like you are saving money. But the jacket was never worth $200 to begin with. The retailer set that anchor specifically so $89 would feel like a steal. If the tag just said $89, you might think twice.

This happens everywhere:

  • Car dealerships start with a high sticker price so the "negotiated" price feels like a win
  • Salary negotiations -- whoever names a number first sets the anchor
  • Restaurants put a $60 steak on the menu so the $28 pasta feels reasonable

Your defense: Before any purchase over $50, ask yourself: "Would I pay this price if I saw it with no context?" Ignore the original price. Judge every price on its own merits.

Loss Aversion

Loss aversion is the psychological tendency to feel the pain of losing money about twice as strongly as the pleasure of gaining the same amount. Losing $100 feels roughly as bad as gaining $200 feels good. Your brain treats losses as threats and gains as nice-to-haves.

How it costs you money: You bought a stock at $50 per share. It drops to $30. Logically, you should evaluate whether the company still has good prospects. But loss aversion makes you hold on, hoping it will "come back" to $50 so you can sell without a loss. Meanwhile, that money could be invested somewhere better. Or worse -- you sell investments that are up because locking in a gain feels safe, while holding losers because selling would make the loss "real."

Loss aversion also keeps people:

  • Paying for gym memberships they never use (canceling feels like "wasting" the signup fee)
  • Staying in bad financial products because switching means admitting a mistake
  • Avoiding investing entirely because a possible loss feels scarier than a likely gain

Your defense: When you feel the urge to hold a losing position, ask: "If I had cash right now instead of this investment, would I buy it today at this price?" If the answer is no, sell. The money is already gone -- your job is to put what is left to its best use.

Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms what you already believe -- while ignoring evidence that contradicts it.

How it costs you money: You hear about a hot cryptocurrency from a friend. You search online and find three articles saying it is the next big thing. You skip the ten articles warning it is a scam. You invest $2,000. Three months later the coin is worth $150.

Confirmation bias also shows up when you:

  • Only read positive reviews after you have already decided to buy something
  • Follow social media accounts that agree with your financial opinions and block those that challenge them
  • Dismiss advice from a financial advisor because it does not match what you want to hear

Your defense: Deliberately seek out the opposing argument. Before any financial decision over $500, search for "[thing you want to do] risks" or "[thing you want to buy] downsides." If you cannot find strong arguments against your plan, you have not looked hard enough.

Emotional Spending: When Feelings Open Your Wallet

Emotional spending is making purchases driven by feelings rather than needs. Everyone does it sometimes. The problem is when it becomes a pattern that quietly derails your financial goals.

Emotional spending is not always about sadness. Any strong emotion can trigger it:

  • Stress: "I had a terrible day. I deserve this $80 dinner."
  • Boredom: Scrolling shopping apps because you have nothing else to do, adding things to your cart "just to look," then buying them.
  • Social pressure: Your friend group is going to a concert you cannot afford. You put it on a credit card because saying no feels awkward.
  • Celebration: "I got a raise! Time for a new car!" (We will talk about this in the next section.)
  • Guilt: Buying expensive gifts you cannot afford because you feel bad about not spending enough time with someone.

How to Recognize Your Triggers

Track your unplanned purchases for two weeks. For each one, write down what you were feeling when you bought it. Patterns will emerge fast. Maybe you always online shop late at night when you are tired. Maybe you spend more on weekends after a stressful work week.

The 24-Hour Rule

Here is a simple technique that saves the average person hundreds of dollars a month: for any unplanned purchase over $30, wait 24 hours. Do not add it to your cart. Do not "save it for later." Close the app or walk away. If you still want it tomorrow with the same intensity, consider buying it.

Research suggests that 60-70% of the time, you will not go back. The emotional impulse fades, and you realize you did not actually need it.

Healthier Alternatives

When you feel the urge to spend emotionally, try substituting with a free or cheap activity:

  • Stressed? Go for a walk, call a friend, or exercise
  • Bored? Start a project, read a book, or learn something new
  • Lonely? Reach out to someone rather than filling the gap with stuff
  • Celebrating? Enjoy the moment -- post about it, cook a nice meal at home, or plan a future reward that fits your budget

The goal is not to never spend money on fun. It is to make those purchases decisions instead of reactions.

Lifestyle Inflation: The Raise That Leaves You Broke

Lifestyle inflation (also called lifestyle creep) happens when your spending increases every time your income increases. You earn more, so you spend more -- and your savings rate stays flat or even shrinks.

Here is how it typically plays out for a young professional:

The Salary Increase Trap

Imagine you earn $50,000 per year and take home about $3,300 per month after taxes. Your expenses are $2,800 and you save $500 per month. Life is tight, but manageable.

Then you get promoted to $70,000 per year. Take-home jumps to roughly $4,500 per month. Here is what happens next for most people:

  • "I deserve a nicer apartment." Rent goes from $1,200 to $1,700.
  • "I can afford a car payment now." New expense: $450/month.
  • "I should upgrade my wardrobe for the new role." Clothing budget doubles.
  • "I'll eat out more -- I'm busy and I've earned it." Food spending climbs $300/month.
  • Subscriptions creep up. A premium gym, streaming bundles, meal kits.

Before you know it, expenses have risen from $2,800 to $4,200. Savings stayed at roughly $300. You earn $20,000 more per year but save less than before.

The 50% Rule for Raises

When you get a raise, commit to saving at least 50% of the after-tax increase. If your take-home pay goes up $1,200 per month, put $600 toward savings, debt payoff, or investing before you adjust your lifestyle. You still get to enjoy more money. You just do not let it all evaporate.

This one habit -- applied consistently across a career -- is the difference between retiring at 55 and retiring at 70.

Financial Scams: They Are Coming for Your Money

A financial scam is any scheme designed to steal your money through deception. Scams are not just for "gullible" people. They are engineered by professionals who study human psychology. Young adults are increasingly targeted because they are active online, may be new to financial products, and are less likely to verify unfamiliar situations.

Americans lost over $10 billion to fraud in 2023, and adults ages 20-29 reported losses more frequently than any other age group under 70. Let that sink in: young adults are among the most common victims.

Common Scam Types Targeting Young Adults

Scam Type How It Works Red Flags
Fake job offers You receive an offer for a remote job you never applied for. They send you a check to "buy equipment," then ask you to send back the difference. The check bounces. Unsolicited offer; asks you to deposit and forward money; vague company details
Investment/crypto scams Someone guarantees 10-50% monthly returns on crypto or forex trading. Early "investors" get paid with later investors' money (Ponzi scheme). Guaranteed returns; pressure to recruit others; unlicensed platform; returns too consistent
Romance scams Someone builds an online relationship over weeks or months, then asks for money for an emergency, travel, or investment opportunity. Never meets in person; relationship moves fast; always has a reason they cannot video call; asks for money or crypto
Fake rental listings A listing shows a great apartment at below-market rent. The "landlord" asks for a deposit before you can tour it. The listing is stolen from a real one. Price too low for the area; cannot tour before paying; landlord is "out of town"; asks for wire transfer or gift cards
Student loan forgiveness scams A company promises to get your loans forgiven for an upfront fee. They either do nothing or file paperwork you could file yourself for free. Charges upfront fees; guarantees forgiveness; asks for your FSA ID and password
Overpayment scams Someone "accidentally" sends you too much money via Venmo, Zelle, or check, then asks you to send back the extra. The original payment is fraudulent. Unexpected payment from a stranger; urgency to return funds quickly

The Universal Red Flags

Regardless of the specific scam, almost all share these warning signs:

  • Urgency: "You must act NOW or lose this opportunity."
  • Secrecy: "Don't tell anyone about this deal."
  • Too good to be true: Guaranteed high returns, free money, or below-market prices.
  • Unusual payment methods: Gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or money orders. Legitimate businesses do not ask for payment in iTunes gift cards.
  • Emotional manipulation: Flattery, fear, romantic interest, or threats.

If you encounter any of these, stop and verify independently. Call the company directly using a number from their official website (not the one in the message). Ask a trusted friend or family member. There is no legitimate financial opportunity that disappears if you take 24 hours to think about it.

Phishing: The Bait in Your Inbox

Phishing is a type of scam where attackers send fake messages (emails, texts, or social media DMs) pretending to be a trusted organization to trick you into revealing personal information like passwords, Social Security numbers, or bank details.

The name comes from "fishing" -- casting out bait and waiting for someone to bite. Modern phishing is sophisticated. It no longer comes from a Nigerian prince. It comes from emails that look exactly like your bank, your employer, or the IRS.

How to Spot Phishing

  • Check the sender's actual email address. The display name might say "Chase Bank" but the address is something like support@chasebank-secure-login.com instead of @chase.com.
  • Hover over links before clicking. On a computer, hover your mouse over any link to see where it actually goes. On a phone, press and hold. If the URL looks weird, do not click.
  • Look for urgency and threats. "Your account will be closed in 24 hours" or "Unusual activity detected -- verify immediately." Real companies give you time.
  • Watch for generic greetings. "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name.
  • Check for spelling and grammar errors. Professional organizations proofread their communications.
  • Never enter credentials from a link in an email. If your bank emails you, open a new browser tab and go to your bank's website directly.

What to Do If You Click a Phishing Link

  1. Do not enter any information. Close the page immediately.
  2. If you already entered credentials, change that password right away -- and any other accounts using the same password.
  3. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on the affected account.
  4. Run an antivirus scan on your device.
  5. Monitor your bank and credit card statements for unauthorized charges.
  6. Report the phishing attempt to the real organization and to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov.

Identity Theft: When Someone Becomes You

Identity theft occurs when someone uses your personal information -- name, Social Security number, credit card numbers, or other identifying data -- to commit fraud. They might open credit cards in your name, file tax returns to steal your refund, or drain your bank accounts.

Identity theft is not a minor inconvenience. Victims spend an average of 200 hours and significant money resolving it. Prevention is far easier than recovery.

Protection Steps You Should Take Today

  1. Freeze your credit at all three bureaus. This is the single most powerful step. A credit freeze (free at Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) prevents anyone from opening new credit accounts in your name. You can temporarily lift the freeze when you need to apply for credit. Do this even if you are 19 and think nobody wants your credit. Especially if you are 19 -- thin credit files are easy targets.

  2. Use unique, strong passwords for every financial account. Use a password manager (Bitwarden is free; 1Password and Dashlane are solid paid options). Never reuse passwords. If one site gets breached, attackers try those credentials on banking sites immediately.

  3. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) everywhere. Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy) rather than SMS when possible. SIM-swapping attacks can intercept text message codes.

  4. Monitor your credit reports. You can check your credit reports for free at AnnualCreditReport.com. Review them at least once a year for accounts you did not open.

  5. Shred documents with personal information. Bank statements, tax documents, pre-approved credit offers -- shred them before discarding.

  6. Be careful with your Social Security number. Very few organizations actually need it. Your gym does not. Your landlord probably does not. Ask why it is needed and whether an alternative ID works.

  7. Set up account alerts. Enable notifications for all transactions on your bank accounts and credit cards. If someone charges $500 at a store in another state, you will know within seconds.

If You Become a Victim

  • File a report at IdentityTheft.gov -- this creates a recovery plan and pre-fills letters and forms for you.
  • Place a fraud alert on your credit reports (different from a freeze; a freeze is still better for prevention).
  • File a police report for documentation.
  • Contact every affected financial institution directly.

Social Media Finance Misinformation

Social media misinformation refers to false or misleading financial advice spread on platforms like TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and X (formerly Twitter). Some of it is from well-meaning people who are simply wrong. Some of it is from people deliberately misleading you to sell courses, products, or investments.

Social media has democratized financial education in many positive ways. But it has also created an environment where a 22-year-old with a ring light and no credentials can reach millions of people with dangerous advice.

Social Media Finance Red Flags

Red Flag What It Sounds Like The Reality
Guaranteed returns "This strategy returns 30% per month, guaranteed" No legitimate investment guarantees returns. Not stocks, not real estate, not crypto.
Get rich quick "I made $50K in a week with this one trick" Wealth builds slowly. Anyone promising fast riches is selling something.
Selling a course as the "real" product "My free content is great, but the $997 course is where the real secrets are" If they made so much money investing, why do they need to sell a course? The course IS their business model.
No credentials or transparency "Trust me, I've been doing this for years" Look for verifiable credentials (CFP, CPA, CFA). Ask where their income actually comes from.
Creating urgency "This opportunity won't last -- join my Discord NOW" Legitimate investments do not have countdown timers.
Cherry-picked results Showing only winning trades, never losses Every investor has losses. If someone only shows wins, they are hiding the full picture.
Tax advice from non-professionals "This one weird LLC trick eliminates your taxes" Tax law is complex. Bad advice can result in IRS penalties. Get tax advice from a CPA or enrolled agent.

How to Evaluate Financial Advice Online

Before taking any financial advice from social media, ask these questions:

  1. Who is this person? Do they have verifiable credentials? Are they registered with FINRA (you can check at BrokerCheck.finra.org)?
  2. How do they make money? If their income comes from selling courses or affiliate links rather than the strategy they teach, that is a conflict of interest.
  3. Are they showing the full picture? Wins and losses? Risks and rewards? Or only the highlight reel?
  4. Can I verify this independently? Cross-reference with established sources like Investopedia, the SEC website, or a fee-only financial advisor.
  5. Does this pass the common sense test? If a strategy were truly making people 30% monthly returns, hedge funds with PhD mathematicians would already be doing it.

Where Your Money Should Go

Now that you understand the mental traps and external threats, here is how these lessons connect to your overall financial plan. Follow this priority order:

  1. Build a $1,000 starter emergency fund. This buffer protects you from making panic decisions when surprise expenses hit. Without it, every flat tire becomes a credit card balance, and loss aversion keeps you paying minimums forever.

  2. Get your full employer 401(k) match. Do not let loss aversion ("I can't afford to lose that money from my paycheck") cost you free money. The employer match is a guaranteed 50-100% return. No scam can offer that legitimately.

  3. Pay off high-interest debt (over 7%). Confirmation bias can keep you rationalizing credit card debt. "I need to keep this card for emergencies." No. Your emergency fund handles that. Pay off the debt.

  4. Build your emergency fund to 3-6 months of expenses. A fully funded emergency fund is the best defense against emotional spending during a crisis and against scammers who exploit financial desperation.

  5. Max out a Roth IRA ($7,000/year in 2025). Do not let present bias ("I'll start next year") steal your twenties -- the most powerful compounding years you will ever have.

  6. Invest in low-cost index funds. Confirmation bias loves to tell you that you can pick winning stocks. The data says otherwise. Broad index funds beat most active managers over time.

  7. Pay off medium-interest debt (4-7%). Steady progress. No drama.

  8. Save for specific goals. Now you have earned the right to lifestyle upgrades -- intentionally, not through lifestyle inflation.

Common Mistakes

These are the most frequent errors young adults make in the areas covered by this chapter:

  • Assuming scams only happen to "stupid" people. Scams are designed to exploit emotions, not intelligence. Overconfidence makes you a better target, not a worse one.

  • Letting lifestyle inflation eat every raise. The 50% rule (save at least half of every raise) is simple, powerful, and almost nobody does it without a deliberate plan.

  • Ignoring small recurring charges. Anchoring bias makes a $14.99/month subscription feel insignificant. But five of them add up to $900 per year. Audit your subscriptions quarterly.

  • Taking financial advice from social media without verification. A viral TikTok is not a financial plan. Cross-reference everything with established sources.

  • Not freezing your credit. A credit freeze is free, takes ten minutes, and is the single most effective identity theft prevention tool. There is no good reason not to do it.

  • Making financial decisions when emotional. Angry, sad, excited, or stressed -- none of these are good states for deciding where your money goes. Use the 24-hour rule.

  • Panic-selling investments during market drops. Loss aversion will scream at you to sell when markets fall. Historically, every major drop has been followed by recovery. If your investment thesis has not changed, neither should your strategy.

Key Terms

  • Behavioral Finance: The study of how psychology and emotions influence financial decisions.
  • Cognitive Bias: A systematic error in thinking that affects decisions and judgments. Everyone has them.
  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered when making decisions.
  • Loss Aversion: The tendency to feel losses roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains, leading to irrational financial choices.
  • Confirmation Bias: The tendency to search for and favor information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.
  • Emotional Spending: Making purchases driven by feelings (stress, boredom, celebration, guilt) rather than actual needs.
  • Lifestyle Inflation: The pattern of increasing spending whenever income increases, so that savings rate stays flat or shrinks despite earning more.
  • Financial Scam: Any deceptive scheme designed to steal money or personal information from victims.
  • Phishing: A scam using fake emails, texts, or messages that impersonate trusted organizations to steal personal information or credentials.
  • Identity Theft: The crime of using someone else's personal information (name, Social Security number, credit card numbers) to commit fraud.
  • Social Media Misinfo: False or misleading financial advice spread on social platforms, often by unqualified individuals seeking to sell courses or promote bad investments.
